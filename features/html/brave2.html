Chapter Two

With two sets of eyes on him now, Draco gulped. But–

“Who are you?” tumbled from his lips, because however much Lookalike appeared to be an older version of him, Draco was no longer sure that was actually the case.

Lookalike shot Potter a pointed glance. It was half-accusation, half-amusement. Fond amusement, Draco realised, feeling stunned.

“You didn’t explain anything to him, either, did you?” said Lookalike, sounding long-suffering; but his amusement won out even as he spoke. His lips twitched at the corners.

“Me? Explain? Why would I do that?” Potter wasn’t even pretending to be contrite, still smiling.

“Merlin.”

“Oh, very well,” said Potter, with the air of someone graciously acquiescing to an unreasonable demand.

Lookalike rolled his eyes as Potter transferred his gaze to Draco, and began to explain.

“As I’m sure you’ve worked out by now, I’m Harry Potter. But I am also Merlin Emrys. After Arthur’s death and the fall of the kingdom nearly a thousand years ago, I performed a spell that would allow myself and King Arthur to be reborn in Britain’s hour of greatest need. It worked.”

Draco tried to take in the enormity of that idea. He wasn’t sure whether he believed it.

But he wasn’t sure that he didn’t believe it, either, which was distressing in its implications.

“This Draco is also King Arthur Pendragon, King of Britain through right of magic,” Potter continued blithely, as though he wasn’t giving Draco an existential crisis. “He currently rules wizarding Britain, and works in consultation with the non-magical Queen. Any questions?”

Draco wasn’t sure that it was wise to ask anything at all. But curiosity and a sense of self-preservation meant that he needed to know.

“The Dark Lord…”

“No longer exists in this universe,” said Lookalike, his expression turning scary again.

Draco thought about the fact that Lookalike was allegedly the muggle king out of the ancient legends. Draco found, to his consternation, that he believed it.

Draco wasn’t sure what to make of that fact. Everyone in the wizarding world grew up knowing a great deal about Merlin the Magnificent, who had flouted the rules of magic simply by existing…

…but Draco couldn’t remember anyone, growing up, who had really cared about the muggle king that Merlin had allegedly spent much of his time protecting. Why would they? Wizards didn’t bother themselves with the antics of the muggles.

Looking at Lookalike – at King Arthur – this suddenly seemed like a grave oversight. Particularly as this King Arthur was, presumably, now a wizard.

Draco wracked his brain for information, any information, that he might know about King Arthur Pendragon, aside from the man’s name. He couldn’t think of anything.

He tried, instead, to think about the idea that the Dark Lord didn’t exist, here. Tried to tell himself that he was safe.

It didn’t work. Draco wasn’t really surprised by that. He’d been far too afraid for far too long to just let go of that emotion now.

Besides which – Potter, Merlin, whatever he wanted to be called – was still firmly holding onto Draco’s arm. So Draco wasn’t sure how safe he really was.

He swallowed.

“You said that he’s a Marked Death Eater?” asked King Arthur, transferring his gaze back to Merlin again.

“Unwillingly Marked, but yes.”

“How can you tell he was unwilling?” King Arthur asked.

“It’s a vassal’s Mark, given by a supposed Lord,” said Merlin. “But the vassal-Lord bond is supposed to be a willing one, see? The depth of loyalty creates the strength of the bond, cementing the Mark in place. But without that loyalty – without willingly giving up one’s autonomy – the Mark is removable. It’s difficult, to be sure, but doable.”

Draco’s treacherous heart leaped at that pronouncement. He promptly directed his mind onto safer tracks.

Merlin didn’t seem to mind that he had to keep explaining things. He’d fallen easily into a professor’s lecturing cadence. But then, if the past twenty minutes had been any indication, he was probably used to it.

Draco was starting to suspect that Merlin was the cleverest person in the room, or at least the most knowledgeable. The awareness that this was Potter made him boggle, even though it was difficult to forget – what with Merlin wearing an older version of Potter’s face.

Potter had always been so ignorant. Seeing him otherwise was… strange, to say the least.

Something niggled at the back of Draco’s brain. It felt uncomfortably like a growing awareness that the Potter of his world might have turned out to be more like Merlin, if only he hadn’t been raised by muggles and fighting for his life half the time.

Draco was also uncomfortably aware that his own actions probably hadn’t helped with Potter’s lack of… well, Draco didn’t have the exact word for it. The closest he could come to was growth.

Draco had been aware of a lot of things lately that he hadn’t been before, there at the back of his mind where he tried not to think about things. It made him feel small, and ashamed.

King Arthur steepled his fingers, his eyes on Draco’s face.

“Do you want it removed?”

Draco thought longingly of living in a world where things might be normal, without death and distraction and the Dark Lord’s brand on his arm.

But if the Dark Lord ever found out about this conversation…

“No,” said Draco, because that was the answer expected of any loyal Death Eater. And of course Draco was loyal. That didn’t come into question. Torture and death awaited traitors.

“Hmm.” King Arthur’s eyes narrowed. He glanced at Merlin. “Can we send him back?”

Draco’s heart plummeted at the thought of returning to his universe, even though he tried to tell himself that that was what he wanted.

“Eventually,” said Merlin. His eyes were still on Draco’s face, and his expression was thoughtful.

Draco tried to blank his own expression so that Merlin couldn’t discern anything from it.

“Eventually?” King Arthur demanded. Finally, Merlin looked away from Draco.

“Yes, eventually. But returning him means opening a portal between worlds and finding the correct one, which won’t be easy. Could take me a while.”

King Arthur sighed in annoyance.

“So what do we do with him?”

“Oh, I thought I’d babysit,” said Merlin, smiling. It took Draco a moment to realise what Merlin meant.

When he did, he flushed.

“I’m seventeen,” Draco said aloud, over the fear, because he still had some dignity left. “Not a child.”

“And you’re clearly so grown up,” Merlin said soothingly. It only increased Draco’s annoyance.

“Has anyone ever told you you’re insufferable?” he snapped, because it didn’t seem like these two would kill him just for speaking out – and the pressure of keeping his thoughts to himself was terrible, some days.

It was King Arthur who answered for him.

“Every day,” the King said dryly.

“You two are merely jealous of my sparkling personality,” said Merlin, back to being cheerful. “Now, come along, Young Draco. Let’s–”

But before he could finish his sentence, the door opened. Draco glanced over, even as Merlin and King Arthur turned their heads.

It was Granger, but not as Draco had ever seen her. She was wearing muggle clothing that even to Draco’s eyes (and Draco knew nothing of muggle fashions) appeared stylish and expensive, and she was carrying a tall stack of papers in her arms.

The Granger that Draco was acquainted with had always had hair that looked like it was trying to escape Granger’s head, an untameable mess, but this Granger’s hair was sleek and neat and tied back into an elegant knot behind her head. Between that and the subtle presence of the cosmetics she’d applied to her face, she looked almost like a different person.

She was already talking as she walked in, but the moment her eyes landed on Draco, Merlin and King Arthur, her words came to a halt. She stopped dead.

“Ah, Hermione,” Merlin greeted her, soft and rueful. “How was the meeting with the Prime Minister?”

But Granger was busy staring at Draco, her mouth hanging open. A second later, her jaw snapped shut.

“Harry,” she said, her voice accusing, and her gaze more than a little frightening. “What did you do?”

“Why does everyone always blame me for these things?”

“Harry –” Granger began, beginning to bristle.

“Experience,” said King Arthur at the same time, his voice wry.

“–if you don’t explain this minute, I will hit you with these papers. All of them. And you can clean up the mess afterwards. I don’t care how long it takes you to compile all the pages.”

Draco eyed the stack of paperwork, wondering if she meant it.

Merlin obviously didn’t wonder.

“Alright, alright,” he said, in placation. “Just–” And he drew his wand.

Before Draco could do more than tense, Merlin tapped Draco’s wrist with his wand, letting go of the grip he had around Draco’s upper arm.

There was a tingling sensation, like an invisible thread had drawn tight around Draco’s wrist before it disappeared.

Like Merlin had just cast an unknown spell on him. Draco tried not to panic.

“There we go,” said Merlin, looking satisfied. “All done.”

“Merlin,” said King Arthur. “Harry. What did you just do to him?”

Draco rather wanted to know that himself.

“Tether spell,” said Merlin. “He can’t leave any room I happen to be in, and if we happen to be outside, he can’t stray more than twelve feet away.”

“And if you forget, and apparate without him?” King Arthur sounded morbidly curious.

“Then he’ll be dragged alongside me to my destination, obviously.”

“But who is he?” said Granger, while King Arthur sarcastically mouthed the word obviously to himself. It was reassuring to see that even he had limits to how much of Merlin’s behaviour he could tolerate.

“He’s a Draco Malfoy from a world where Voldemort ascended to power,” said Merlin, ignoring King Arthur. “He’s a Death Eater, unwillingly Marked. Not sure exactly what his ideology is, though, so be careful. Which reminds me…” He turned his head, and met Draco’s gaze.

Eyes the green of new leaves pierced Draco’s soul, stripping him bare of every shield and barrier that he possessed, leaving him helplessly exposed to that self-shattering gaze. Draco had a sense of something vast and impossibly powerful lurking behind those eyes, somewhere just beyond Draco’s conscious awareness.

Merlin’s voice as he spoke was soft, easy, and carried a deadly threat that Draco was certain the other wizard wouldn’t hesitate to carry out.

“Harm anyone while you are here in this world and you will very much regret it, do you understand?”

Draco found enough strength to nod, mesmerised by sheer terror.

Suddenly the menace that Merlin emanated was gone, as though it had never been.

Merlin turned away, breaking mental contact, leaving Draco to gather together the tattered remnants of his occlumency shields.

Merlin hadn’t hurt him – unlike the Dark Lord, who always took sadistic, painful enjoyment in tearing down Draco’s shields, piece by piece – but he’d gone through Draco’s mental defences like they were nothing, and with expert precision. Draco had no doubt that Merlin could have broken his mind, if that had been his aim.

But he hadn’t. Why he hadn’t, when it would have been so easy for him, Draco didn’t understand.

He took back everything he’d thought about King Arthur being scary. At least King Arthur was only a wizard. Merlin was something else. But then… he had mentioned demonic heritage, earlier…

Granger was frowning, the expression shading from need-to-know into genuine concern. Draco realised, too late, that he was trembling. He tried to stop, but couldn’t.

“Are you alright?” she asked Draco.

“Not really,” Draco managed. The world was turning dark around the edges, and everything else was wobbling. “I think…”

But he never had a chance to say what he thought. The darkness swallowed him up, and the last thing Draco was aware of was a sensation of falling, right before his consciousness was snuffed out like a candle flame.
